# Intellectual Property Protection & Accreditation

## Establishing Priority (Do These NOW)

### 1. arXiv Preprint (most important)
Upload to arXiv.org before journal submission. This:
- Creates a **timestamped, immutable public record** of your findings
- Establishes priority with a DOI-like identifier (arXiv:YYMM.NNNNN)
- Is standard practice in physics, mathematics, and econophysics
- Does NOT prevent journal publication (all target journals accept arXiv preprints)
- Category: `q-fin.ST` (Statistical Finance) or `physics.data-an`

**Steps:**
1. Create account at arxiv.org
2. Get endorsement (may need an existing arXiv author to endorse you for q-fin)
3. Upload PDF + LaTeX source
4. Receive arXiv ID within 1-2 business days

### 2. ORCID (persistent researcher identity)
Register at orcid.org. This is your permanent researcher ID that follows you across institutions, journals, and grants. Link it to your arXiv submissions and journal papers.

### 3. GitHub Commit History (already done)
Your git commit history with timestamps provides provenance for the code and methodology. The repo is public at github.com/mhdk1602/Matlab---fractal-modelling. This is supplementary evidence of when the work was done.

### 4. Zenodo DOI for the Code
Zenodo (zenodo.org) integrates with GitHub. With one click, it archives a snapshot of your repo and assigns a permanent DOI. Cite this DOI in your paper under "Data Availability" or "Code Availability." This makes your code citable and permanently archived.

## Copyright

- **Copyright is automatic upon creation.** You own the copyright to your manuscript from the moment you write it.
- When you submit to a journal, you typically sign a **copyright transfer agreement** (CTA) or a **license to publish** depending on the journal.
- Elsevier (Physica A, Chaos Solitons & Fractals): you transfer copyright but retain rights to post on arXiv, use in your thesis, share preprints.
- If you pay for open access: you retain copyright under a Creative Commons license (CC-BY).

## Accreditation

- **Sole authorship** is perfectly appropriate here. You conceived the research question, wrote the code, ran the analysis, and will write the paper.
- If anyone provided substantial intellectual contribution (not just tools or data), they should be acknowledged or co-authored per your field's norms.
- **Acknowledgments section**: thank anyone who reviewed drafts, provided feedback, or whose tools you used (e.g., "The author thanks the developers of nolds and MFDFA for their open-source implementations.")
- **AI disclosure**: Some journals now require disclosure of AI tool usage. Check the target journal's policy. Standard phrasing: "Computational tools including Claude (Anthropic) were used for code development assistance. All intellectual contributions, research design, analysis, and interpretation are the author's own."
